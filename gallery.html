The Eternal Presence

The Eternal Presence
Wifredo Lam, The Eternal Presence (An Homage to Alejandro García Caturla), 1944,
oil and pastel over papier mâché and chalk ground on bast fiber fabric, 85 1/4 x 77 1/8 inches (Rhode Island School of Design Museum)

The Eternal Presence (Homage to Alejandro García Caturla) is a painting by Wifredo Lam, created in 1944. The work is an homage to the Cuban composer Alejandro García Caturla, who was a friend of Lam's and a fellow member of the Grupo Orígenes, a literary and artistic movement that sought to promote Cuban culture and identity.

The painting includes three human-animal hybrid creatures in a dense composition. The number three not only represents the holy trinity in Christianity, but also, in Chinese philosophy, symbolizes birth and new beginnings emerging from chaos. Lam explained that the equine-female figure on the left (reminiscent of the horse-headed woman he painted in other works) represents the paradise that foreigners seek in Cuba, a land of pleasures and sickly-sweet music, and many scholars have interpreted her to be Oshun, the Orisha or deity of love in Santería, while the other two figures appear to represent the warrior deities Elegua and Ogun.

An additional layer of meaning comes from the significance of the horse in Chinese culture (which Lam had begun to revisit and study upon his return to Cuba), in which the animal is valued for its military usefulness. As well, horses in Chinese visual representation frequently carry spiritual meaning, believed to guide individuals on heavenly journeys. The work's layered meanings and myriad cultural references are reflected in the composition. The limbs and bodies of the hidden figures twist and overlap with one another to a dizzying, confusing effect, which is further enhanced by the monochromatic color palette that flattens background and foreground. Emerging from this complex pictorial field, the figures' faces meet the viewer's gaze directly, even confrontationally.

Lam intended the painting to serve as a commentary on the corruption of Cuba by imperialist powers, and dedicated the work to one of his major artistic influences, Cuban composer and judge Alejandro García Caturla, who was murdered on the morning of November 12, 1940, by the defendant in a trial he was meant to oversee later that day. Only 34 years old at the time of his death, Caturla was already a leader in Afro-Cubanism (an interdisciplinary movement aimed at increasing the recognition of African culture in Cuban society), blending both European and African musical elements in his compositions.

Satan

Satan
Wifredo Lam, Satan, 1942. Gouache on paper. Dimensions: 41 7/8 x 34" (106.4 x 86.4 cm)

The painting, Satan, by Wifredo Lam features a central female figure in the foreground. This figure is a combination of hybridized figures with distorted and elongated bodies. The figures are arranged in a chaotic manner, with limbs and body parts interlocking and overlapping.

One of the most striking aspects of the painting is the use of transmogrification, a device frequently used by Lam in his work. In Satan, the figure's testicle "chin" is a clear example of this technique. Lam was inspired by indigenous American and African ritual objects, which often feature transmogrification of body parts to suggest magical metamorphosis.

The central figure in Satan is also partly inspired by the femme-cheval or horse-headed woman of the Santeria cult. This is reflected in the figure's elongated face and the suggestion of a horse's head in her features.

The use of animal symbolism is another prominent feature of the painting. Lam's use of horse imagery can be linked to his Afro-Cuban roots and his interest in the mythology and rituals of Santería, a syncretic religion that developed in Cuba among enslaved Africans and their descendants.

Horses in Lam’s Works

In Santeria, the horse is a powerful symbol that represents the Orisha (deities) Oya and Ogun, among others. The Orishas are believed to be intermediaries between humans and the supreme deity, and their possession is an important part of Santeria rituals. When a practitioner is possessed by an Orisha, they are said to be "ridden" by the spirit, and the experience is often described as being like a horse being ridden by a rider.

Lam, who was deeply influenced by African and Afro-Caribbean culture and mythology, often incorporated horse imagery into his work as a way to explore themes of power, transformation, and spirituality. In many of his paintings, horses are depicted with elongated bodies, distorted features, and multiple heads, suggesting a merging of different entities or a transformation into a powerful being. In works like Satan, Lam merges horses with other forms or figures, creating hybrid creatures that blur the boundaries between human and animal, and between the natural and the supernatural.

The Jungle

The Jungle
Wifredo Lam, The Jungle, 1942-43, gouache on paper mounted on canvas, 94-1/4 x 90-1/2 inches / 239.4 x 229.9 cm (The Museum of Modern Art)

Wifredo Lam's painting The Jungle was created in 1943. It is an oil on canvas work that showcases Lam's signature style that blends Cubism, Surrealism, and Afro-Cuban imagery.

The painting depicts a lush tropical jungle that is teeming with various elements, both animate and inanimate. Several masked figures protrude out of the thick sugarcane field. These figures appear to be part of the jungle itself, with vines and leaves growing out of their bodies. During the 1940s in Cuba, sugarcane was a major industry and a crucial part of the country's economy. The production of sugarcane required a significant amount of labor, which was often provided by Afro-Cuban and Haitian migrant workers. These workers were subjected to poor working conditions, low wages, and discrimination, similar to the experience of Black Americans in the cotton industry in the American South before the Civil War. This exploitation of labor is a theme that is explored in this painting via the sugarcane imagery.

The painting's title, The Jungle, hints at the idea of a wild and untamed place, full of life and danger. This theme is further emphasized by the various animals that are scattered throughout the painting. There are snakes, lizards, birds, and insects, all of which add to the sense of movement and activity in the jungle.

One element of the painting that stands out is the use of hidden figures. Upon closer inspection, one can see that there are faces and other figures hidden within the leaves and vines. This technique creates a sense of mystery and intrigue, as if the jungle is hiding secrets that can only be uncovered by those who take the time to look closely.

Lam employs a wide range of hues, from deep greens and browns to bright yellows and oranges. The colors are used to create a sense of depth and dimensionality, with the darker tones receding into the background and the brighter tones popping out in the foreground.

In the essay "Strivings of the Negro People," W.E.B. Du Bois talks about the idea of double consciousness, which refers to the way in which Black Americans must navigate two different worlds – the world of Blackness and the world of whiteness. This concept is relevant to "The Jungle" because Lam's use of hidden figures and Afro-Cuban imagery can be seen as a way of highlighting the otherness of Blackness.

In The Jungle, the use of masked figures serves to convey the idea of spiritual possession and ritualistic practices. The masks that adorn the figures are reminiscent of African and Afro-Cuban spiritual traditions, which Lam was deeply influenced by.

The figures in the painting are depicted in a state of frenzy, their bodies contorted and their limbs elongated. The use of masks, along with the distorted bodies, conveys the idea that these figures are not just human, but rather possessed by otherworldly forces. This idea of possession is a common theme in African and Afro-Cuban religions, where spirits are believed to inhabit the bodies of humans during ritualistic ceremonies.


Furthermore, the masked figures in The Jungle can also be interpreted as a commentary on the dehumanization of black people during colonialism and slavery. The masks serve as a way to conceal the true identity of the figures, further perpetuating the idea of them being seen as "other" or exotic. This idea of otherness and dehumanization is a common theme in the works of Lam and other Afro-Cuban artists, who sought to challenge the dominant narratives of Western art that perpetuated racist and colonialist ideologies.
